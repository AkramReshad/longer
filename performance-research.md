# Performance research

Longer connects sexual performance and physical performance through hydration. The literature below supports the biological and cultural premise; it does not constitute product-specific evidence for the Longer formula.

## Core literature

### Hydration and erectile function

Simon N. Thornton, “Hydration increases tissue perfusion and thus erectile function,” *The Journal of Sexual Medicine* 6, no. 9 (2009): 2621–2622.

- [PubMed PMID 19523036](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19523036/)
- [DOI 10.1111/j.1743-6109.2009.01358.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1743-6109.2009.01358.x)
- Publication type: Comment.
- Relevance: Directly articulates the hydration → tissue perfusion → erectile function mechanism.
- Limitation: Not a controlled intervention trial of an electrolyte product.

### Serum sodium and sexual function

“Hyponatremia, hypernatremia and impairment of functional, psychological and sexual domains,” *Endocrine* (2023).

- [PubMed PMID 37884780](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37884780/)
- Population: 3,340 community-dwelling European men aged 40–79.
- Finding: After adjustment, serum sodium above 147 mmol/L was associated with worse erectile function and greater concern about sexual dysfunction.
- Relevance: Connects hydration-related physiology with sexual function in a large male cohort.
- Limitation: Cross-sectional association; it does not establish that electrolyte supplementation improves erections.

### Dietary minerals and erectile dysfunction

Chen-Yuan Deng, Xin-Peng Ke, and Xu-Guang Guo, “Dietary calcium, phosphorus, and potassium intake associated with erectile dysfunction in the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) 2001 to 2004,” *PLOS ONE* 19, no. 2 (2024): e0297129.

- [PubMed PMID 38381721](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38381721/)
- [Open-access full text, PMCID PMC10880986](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10880986/)
- [DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0297129](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0297129)
- Population: 3,556 U.S. men in an NHANES cross-sectional analysis.
- Finding: Higher reported potassium, calcium, and phosphorus intake was associated with lower prevalence of erectile dysfunction after multivariable adjustment.
- Relevance: Provides a direct observational link between dietary mineral intake and erectile function.
- Limitation: Cross-sectional dietary data cannot establish causation or product efficacy.

## Research-backed creative territory

The literature supports using these ideas as the intellectual spine of the campaign:

- Hydration affects circulation.
- Circulation affects performance.
- Electrolytes participate in normal vascular, muscular, and nervous-system function.
- Sexual and athletic performance are expressions of the same body.
- Dehydration can compromise performance in more ways than one.

## Evidence still worth generating

A Longer-specific study could examine mildly dehydrated healthy men in a randomized crossover design comparing Longer, water, and placebo. Useful endpoints would include hydration markers, time to rehydration, perceived physical readiness, validated sexual-function measures, and erection-quality measures appropriate to the study setting.

The strongest eventual product claim should follow the outcome the study actually demonstrates.
